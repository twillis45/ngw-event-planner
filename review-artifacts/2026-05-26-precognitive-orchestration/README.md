# Sprint 30 — Pre-Cognitive Orchestration

Destroy the last remaining "UI" feeling. The environment stops being designed and starts being inevitable. The planner enters an operational reality that already existed before they arrived — not a crafted orchestration system.

## What this is

This is NOT interface design. This is NOT layout refinement. This is the creation of cognitive terrain — where operational pressure, institutional memory, temporal erosion, and environmental contamination shape the surface without announcing their presence. The user should subconsciously absorb operational state, not consciously process visual hierarchy.

## Critical philosophy

The product should feel like:
- the fossil record of coordination pressure
- an operationally inhabited environment with history, gravity, wear
- carrying operational weather in your hand (mobile)

NOT:
- beautifully arranged software
- a premium UI system
- an orchestration dashboard
- a crafted design environment

## New systems introduced

### A. Cognitive Fog
Not everything resolves equally. Trusted, stable, historically reliable information dissolves into environmental periphery. The planner discovers operational reality instead of consuming presented hierarchy.

- 5 fog zones: Active (1.0) → Moderate (0.75) → Peripheral (0.45) → Fog (0.2) → Ghost (0.1)
- Fog is earned — only vendors/sequences with operational trust dissolve
- Discovery cognition replaces consumption cognition

### B. Active Environmental Drift
The environment subtly feels mid-process. Spacing distorted by operational pressure. Continuity regions bleeding into adjacent zones. Rhythm perturbed by institutional memory.

- Spacing varies by operational pressure: 3px (gravity well) → 16px (peripheral)
- Item widths vary by attention mass: 890px (pressure) → 740px (absorbed)
- Corner radius varies by temporal erosion: 2px (new/scarred) → 6px (trusted)

### C. Temporal Erosion
Stable systems feel smoothed by time. Fragile systems feel spatially abrasive. First-time vendors are rough (sharp corners, strong borders). Tenth-time vendors are smooth (round corners, no borders). Smoothing is earned, not designed.

- New vendor: 1.0 opacity, 2px corners, 1px amber border, Medium weight
- Trusted vendor: 0.45 opacity, 5px corners, 0.5px border, Regular weight
- Metabolized vendor: 0.18 opacity, 6px corners, no border, Regular weight

### D. Subconscious Attention Redirection
Attention guided by spatial gravity, continuity interruption, rhythm breakage, compression pressure — not by highlighting, borders, or weight contrast. If you can point to WHY something looks important, the hierarchy is too visible.

### E. Operational Fatigue Modeling
The environment metabolizes cognitive load. Post-ceremony areas feel emotionally collapsed. Completed items at 0.2 opacity. Active coordination at 0.7. Later items in deep fog at 0.2. The environment breathes with operational energy expenditure.

### F. Environmental Contamination
Operational pressure spreads. A sound check delay (source: 1.0) contaminates the bridal lineup (0.9), ceremony start (0.85), family photos (0.65), cocktail hour (0.5), and is fully absorbed by reception (0.35). Contamination weakens with distance — shown through opacity, spacing, and border weight.

### G. Invisible Hierarchy
Items have spatial mass without visual emphasis. The gravity well (lighting lock) doesn't "look" more important — spacing compresses approaching it (14→12→10→6→4→2px) and decompresses after (4→8→12→14px). The planner feels its gravity without seeing a priority signal.

### H. Nonlinear Cognitive Flow
Information arranged by operational proximity, not reading order. Eye circulates, hovers, senses, drifts. Three simultaneous zones: ceremony cluster (gravitational center), vendor field (ambient), temporal periphery (fog).

### I. Orchestration Gravity Wells
Critical operational moments bend nearby environment. Phase spacing encodes pressure: TECHNICAL at 4px gaps, CEREMONY at 3px, RECEPTION at 12px, BREAKDOWN at 20px. The timeline's spatial density IS the pressure map.

### J. Atmospheric Memory
Institutional memory becomes climatic. Venue areas carry atmospheric weight from previous events. Cocktail courtyard has a SCAR from March gala bottleneck (0.9 opacity, red border). Kitchen corridor is environmentally invisible after 22 trusted events (0.2 opacity). Memory becomes weather, not data.

### K. Object Death
Cards merge into orchestration terrain. Containers dissolve. Information exists as continuous environmental density. Dense zone (ceremony): 3px gaps, 0.85 opacity. Medium (cocktail): 8px gaps, 0.55 opacity. Sparse (reception): 14px gaps, 0.3 opacity. Ghost (breakdown): 24px gaps, 0.12 opacity. No dividers between zones — the terrain just changes.

### L. Post-Interface State
The product no longer feels like software. Pure density variation — information emerges from and dissolves into the surface. The phone becomes a window into operational weather, not a dashboard showing status.

## Gravity well mechanics

The gravity well is the core spatial mechanism. Critical operational moments create spatial mass:

| Distance from well | Spacing | Opacity | Border | Weight | Corner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Far approach | 14px | 0.2-0.3 | none | Regular | 6px |
| Medium approach | 10px | 0.45 | 0.5px faint | Regular | 5px |
| Near approach | 6px | 0.7 | 1px teal | Medium | 4px |
| Adjacent | 4px | 0.85 | 1px amber | Medium | 3px |
| Well center | 2px | 1.0 | 1.5px amber | Medium | 2px |
| Near departure | 4px | 0.75 | 0.5px teal | Medium | 4px |

## Mobile as primary truth test

Desktop can fake atmosphere. Mobile exposes whether the philosophy is real.

6 mobile surfaces test different aspects:
- **Surface 11**: Gravity well compression (2px ceremony spacing vs 14px fog)
- **Surface 12**: 5-zone cognitive fog (1.0 active to 0.1 ghost)
- **Surface 13**: Temporal erosion (new=rough, trusted=smooth, metabolized=invisible)
- **Surface 14**: Post-event decompression (environmental collapse, silence zones)
- **Surface 15**: Sequence weather (operational pressure as atmospheric condition)
- **Surface 16**: Post-interface terrain (pure density variation, no UI containers)

If mobile still feels like stacked components, the philosophy is fake.

## Drift risks

| Pattern | Risk Level | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Fog → decoration (opacity as style) | HIGH | Fog is earned through operational trust. If opacity doesn't correlate with operational history, it's decorative. |
| Gravity wells → visual emphasis | HIGH | Wells bend space, not draw attention. If a gravity well "looks" important, it's too visible. Spacing compression IS the well. |
| Contamination → alert cascade | HIGH | Contamination is environmental — opacity and spacing propagation, not notification chains. |
| Object death → messiness | MEDIUM | Continuous terrain is structured — density varies predictably by operational state. Chaos is not the goal. |
| Atmospheric memory → history badges | HIGH | Memory is climatic — the planner FEELS venue difficulty without reading it. If memory is informational, it's a feature, not an environment. |
| Temporal erosion → decorative weathering | HIGH | Erosion is operational — smooth surfaces earned through trust, rough surfaces from coordination friction. Not visual texture. |
| Fatigue modeling → wellness features | MEDIUM | The environment metabolizes fatigue through pacing. No stress meters, break reminders, or cognitive load scores. |
| Nonlinear flow → random layout | MEDIUM | Nonlinear doesn't mean unstructured. Information is arranged by operational proximity, which has its own logic. |
| Post-interface → broken design | HIGH | The absence of containers must feel inhabited, not unfinished. Density variation must be perceptible as intentional. |
| Mobile fog → faded UI | HIGH | Mobile fog must feel like operational weather, not a desaturated theme. Active items at full opacity provide contrast. |

## What was intentionally refused

1. Decorative blur / frosted glass / gaussian overlays
2. Opacity as style choice (opacity must encode operational trust)
3. Random misalignment / intentional sloppiness / grunge aesthetic
4. Decorative weathering / aging filters / patina effects
5. Highlighting / glowing borders / attention animations
6. Stress meters / fatigue indicators / wellness dashboards
7. Alert propagation / cascading notifications / risk heat maps
8. Bold/size-based hierarchy / color-coded priority levels
9. Sequential navigation / step-by-step flows / breadcrumbs
10. Visual emphasis / enlarged cards / "important" labels
11. History badges / "last time" notifications / data-driven memory
12. Card-based layouts / discrete containers / widget grids
13. Charts / graphs / dashboards / KPI tiles
14. AI summaries / predictive widgets / smart insights
15. Software aesthetic / UI patterns / app-like behavior

## What is NOT validated

- No real planner has reviewed these surfaces. All content is synthetic.
- Whether cognitive fog reads as "operational trust" or "faded/broken" is unknown.
- Whether gravity wells guide attention through spacing compression or just look inconsistent is untested.
- Whether contamination propagation is perceptible (opacity from 1.0 to 0.35 over 7 items) needs field testing.
- Whether object death (no containers) feels "continuous terrain" or "unstructured mess" is the critical open question.
- Whether temporal erosion (smooth=trusted, rough=new) is perceivable or just looks inconsistent is unknown.
- Whether atmospheric memory (venue areas at different opacities) communicates venue history or just looks uneven is untested.
- Whether the post-interface mobile surface (Surface 16) feels like "operational weather" or "broken phone app" is the ultimate test.
- Mobile surfaces have not been tested with planners during live events.
- None of these surfaces exist in code yet. They are Figma explorations.

## Honest read

This sprint pushes further from conventional interface design than anything in the series. The core innovation is spatial encoding of operational reality: gravity wells that compress spacing around critical moments, cognitive fog that dissolves trusted information into periphery, contamination that propagates pressure through opacity gradients, and object death that replaces containers with continuous density fields.

The risk is proportional to the ambition. Surface 16 (Post-Interface Terrain) is just text at varying opacity with variable spacing. No cards, no containers, no sections. Either it feels like operational weather in your hand, or it feels like a broken app. There is no middle ground.

The gravity well mechanic is the most testable concept: does spacing compression from 14px to 2px approaching a critical item, then decompression back to 14px, actually guide attention? Or does it just look like inconsistent spacing? The contamination mechanic is similarly binary: does a sound check delay at 1.0 opacity propagating to a bridal lineup at 0.9 and ceremony at 0.85 feel like operational pressure spreading? Or does it look like random opacity values?

The mobile surfaces are the honesty test the directive demands. Surface 11 compresses spacing to 2px at the ceremony gravity well. Surface 14 collapses the post-event environment to 0.06 opacity. Surface 16 abandons containers entirely. If a planner holding their phone backstage feels operational weather instead of using a responsive dashboard — the philosophy is real. If they see stacked components — it's fake.

The cognitive fog concept is the most practically valuable: 22-event caterer at 0.2 opacity, first-time videographer at 1.0 opacity. Trust earned over time shapes visual prominence. But whether a planner interprets 0.2 opacity as "trusted and handled" versus "broken UI" determines whether the entire system works or fails.
