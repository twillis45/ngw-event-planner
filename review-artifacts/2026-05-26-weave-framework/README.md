# Weave — Conceptual Orchestration Exploration Engine

Weave is the atmospheric exploration system for NGW Planner OS. It exists to break inherited interface cognition — the involuntary habit of designing software when the goal is designing operational environments.

Weave is NOT a design tool. It is a concept extraction system that generates atmospheric raw material, filters it for operational credibility, and translates survivable concepts into Figma orchestration surfaces.

## 1. Master Weave System — Conceptual Architecture

### What Weave does

Weave generates environmental concepts that no UI design process would produce. Interface design starts with layouts, components, and patterns. Weave starts with operational pressure, institutional memory, and coordination physics — then discovers what surfaces those forces naturally produce.

### The three phases

```
GENERATE → FILTER → TRANSLATE
   ↓          ↓          ↓
 Weave     Concept    Figma
 Prompts   Filters    Surfaces
```

**Generate**: Atmospheric exploration through structured prompts. Produce raw environmental concepts — most will be unusable. That is the point. The unusable concepts break cognitive habits that would otherwise produce conventional UI.

**Filter**: Every concept passes through operational credibility tests. Does this concept encode real coordination pressure? Would a planner walking backstage at an event perceive this as useful? Or is it conceptual art masquerading as design thinking?

**Translate**: Surviving concepts are operationalized into Figma surfaces using the Studio Matte token system. Atmosphere is reduced to specific spacing values, opacity parameters, border treatments, and density variation. If a concept cannot be expressed through the existing token system (canvas, card, border, opacity, spacing, corner radius, font weight), it is not translatable and is discarded.

### Why Weave exists

The design system has evolved through 12+ sprints of doctrine. Each sprint pushed further from conventional interface design:

- Sprint 20: Cognitive architecture
- Sprint 21: Planner language authenticity
- Sprint 22: Visual orchestration language
- Sprint 23: Visual choreography + spatial orchestration
- Sprint 24: Contextual polish systems
- Sprint 25: Living orchestration environment
- Sprint 27: Evolutionary orchestration realism
- Sprint 27A: Amber palette rebalance
- Sprint 30: Pre-cognitive orchestration

The doctrine is now at post-interface territory. The next concepts cannot emerge from conventional design thinking. They require atmospheric exploration that operates outside the design process — then feeds filtered results back into it.

## 2. Exploration Lanes

Exploration lanes are parallel tracks of atmospheric investigation. Each lane has a specific operational question it investigates, specific dangers it must avoid, and specific outputs that feed into Figma translation.

### Lane 1: Operational Gravity

**Question**: How do critical coordination moments distort the spatial environment around them?

**Investigates**: Whether operational pressure creates perceivable spatial effects. Whether a ceremony sequence naturally compresses adjacent scheduling. Whether timing pressure produces environmental density that guides attention without visual hierarchy.

**Goal**: Discover spatial distortion patterns that encode operational importance through proximity effects rather than visual emphasis.

**Dangers**:
- Drifting into physics simulations or force-field visualizations
- Creating beautiful gravitational art instead of operational spacing
- Making gravity visible (gravity should be felt, not seen)
- Over-engineering distortion until it looks designed

**Outputs**: Spacing compression ratios, density gradients, proximity effects — expressed as specific px values and opacity parameters.

**Validation**: Would a planner notice this spacing pattern? Not consciously — but would their eye naturally find the critical moment? If yes, the gravity is working. If the planner can articulate "this section has tighter spacing," the gravity is too visible.

---

### Lane 2: Environmental Memory

**Question**: How does repeated operational experience reshape the coordination surface?

**Investigates**: Whether institutional knowledge can be encoded spatially. Whether a venue used 7 times should feel different from a venue used once. Whether vendor trust produces visual smoothing that is perceptible but not labeled.

**Goal**: Discover memory encoding patterns that feel like naturally accumulated experience rather than data display.

**Dangers**:
- Creating history dashboards or timeline displays
- Adding memory badges, history labels, or "last time" indicators
- Making memory informational rather than atmospheric
- Decorative weathering or aging effects

**Outputs**: Trust-opacity curves, familiarity-corner-radius relationships, experience-density parameters — how specific operational histories map to specific visual treatment values.

**Validation**: Does the planner feel "this venue feels familiar" without reading "7th event here"? If the memory is atmospheric, it works. If it requires reading, it is information display, not environmental memory.

---

### Lane 3: Continuity Fields

**Question**: How does coordination state persist across surface transitions?

**Investigates**: Whether operational pressure carries through when the planner moves between views. Whether ceremony tension affects how the vendor list renders. Whether timing urgency persists as environmental residue.

**Goal**: Discover cross-surface persistence patterns that make the environment feel continuous rather than composed of discrete screens.

**Dangers**:
- Building transition animations or page-flip effects
- Creating visual "threads" that connect screens decoratively
- Making continuity a feature rather than an environmental quality
- Over-engineering persistence until it feels like state management UI

**Outputs**: Residue opacity values, pressure inheritance parameters, temporal carryover mechanics — how a completed ceremony's atmospheric weight affects the cocktail-hour surface.

**Validation**: Does navigating between surfaces feel like moving through a continuous environment? Or like switching between screens? If the planner perceives screens, continuity has failed.

---

### Lane 4: Cognitive Dissolution

**Question**: How do stable, trusted systems become environmentally invisible?

**Investigates**: Whether operational trust can produce visual dissolution. Whether a vendor confirmed for the 22nd time should nearly disappear. Whether information stability naturally reduces cognitive presence.

**Goal**: Discover dissolution curves that map operational trust to visual presence, producing cognitive fog that directs attention to what actually needs it.

**Dangers**:
- Making important information invisible (dissolution is for STABLE items, not all items)
- Using opacity as a style choice rather than an operational encoding
- Creating a "faded" aesthetic that looks like a design decision
- Dissolving too aggressively, making the environment feel empty

**Outputs**: Trust-opacity mapping functions, stability-visibility curves, dissolution thresholds — at what operational trust level does an item begin dissolving?

**Validation**: Can the planner find dissolved information when they need it? Dissolution means peripheral, not hidden. If a planner cannot locate their 22nd-time caterer when specifically looking, dissolution has gone too far.

---

### Lane 5: Pressure Weather

**Question**: How does coordination pressure manifest as environmental atmosphere?

**Investigates**: Whether timing pressure, vendor dependencies, handoff complexity, and ceremony gravity produce perceivable atmospheric conditions. Whether "operational weather" is a useful metaphor or conceptual fiction.

**Goal**: Discover atmospheric encoding patterns that communicate coordination state without status indicators. The planner should feel the weather, not read a forecast.

**Dangers**:
- Creating weather visualizations (clouds, storms, sunshine icons)
- Building atmospheric effects (blur, particle systems, color washes)
- Making weather literal rather than spatial
- Sci-fi environmental rendering

**Outputs**: Pressure-density parameters, atmospheric spacing values, weather-border relationships — how operational weather maps to specific visual treatments using existing tokens.

**Validation**: Does the planner perceive "this feels tense" without seeing a "HIGH PRESSURE" label? If the atmosphere communicates, the weather is working. If it requires explanation, it is conceptual fiction.

---

### Lane 6: Object Mortality

**Question**: How do discrete interface objects dissolve into continuous environmental terrain?

**Investigates**: Whether cards, containers, sections, and dividers can progressively dissolve as the environment matures. Whether information can exist as density variation in a continuous field rather than as content inside boxes.

**Goal**: Discover the boundary between "continuous terrain" and "broken layout." Find the dissolution point where containers disappear but information remains navigable.

**Dangers**:
- Removing all structure (terrain is structured, just not containerized)
- Creating abstract art that is unusable
- Making the dissolution a style rather than an operational consequence
- Losing all wayfinding cues

**Outputs**: Container dissolution thresholds, terrain density parameters, minimum wayfinding requirements — how much structure can dissolve before usability collapses.

**Validation**: Can the planner find what they need? If object death produces a surface where operational tasks are achievable — even if the surface looks nothing like software — it works. If the planner is lost, structure dissolution has exceeded usability.

---

### Lane 7: Mobile Atmospheric Cognition

**Question**: How does a 390px screen become an environmental awareness instrument rather than a responsive dashboard?

**Investigates**: Whether mobile can convey operational weather. Whether gravity wells function in single-column flow. Whether cognitive fog is perceivable at small scale. Whether the phone feels like carrying operational awareness, not using an app.

**Goal**: Discover mobile-native atmospheric patterns that have no desktop equivalent. Mobile is not compressed desktop — it is a different cognitive instrument.

**Dangers**:
- Stacking desktop components vertically
- Creating a "mobile version" of desktop atmosphere
- Making mobile feel like a small dashboard
- Losing atmospheric quality at small scale

**Outputs**: Mobile-specific density patterns, single-column gravity mechanics, touch-scale fog zones, mobile-native temporal compression.

**Validation**: Does the planner feel operational awareness when glancing at their phone backstage? Or do they feel like they opened an app? If the phone feels like a weather instrument, the mobile atmosphere works.

## 3. Weave Prompt Framework

### Prompt architecture

Every Weave exploration prompt follows a modular structure:

```
[OPERATIONAL CONTEXT]
[EXPLORATION TARGET]
[ATMOSPHERIC DIRECTION]
[ANTI-PATTERN CONSTRAINTS]
[OUTPUT SPECIFICATION]
```

### Operational context module

Inject real operational scenarios. Never explore in abstract.

```
OPERATIONAL CONTEXT:
- Event type: [wedding / corporate / ceremony]
- Venue: [name, Nth event here]
- Timing: [days out / event day / post-event]
- Pressure: [calm / building / compressed / critical / decompressing]
- Vendor state: [new / familiar / trusted / metabolized]
- History: [scars, institutional memories, absorbed patterns]
- Planner state: [fresh / coordinating / fatigued / post-event]
```

### Exploration target module

Specify what operational question the exploration investigates.

```
EXPLORATION TARGET:
Investigate how [operational force] shapes [environmental quality]
when [specific operational condition] is present.

Example: Investigate how vendor trust shapes visual dissolution
when 22 events of confirmed reliable service create operational fog.
```

### Atmospheric direction module

Guide toward environmental thinking, away from interface thinking.

```
ATMOSPHERIC DIRECTION:
The exploration should feel like:
- [environmental reference: backstage before performance]
- [spatial reference: air traffic coordination pressure]
- [institutional reference: hotel operations room]

NOT like:
- [anti-reference: SaaS dashboard]
- [anti-reference: design portfolio]
- [anti-reference: futuristic command center]
```

### Anti-pattern constraints module

Explicit prohibitions. These prevent the most common drift patterns.

```
ANTI-PATTERN CONSTRAINTS:
- No cards, tiles, or discrete containers unless operationally justified
- No visual hierarchy through size/weight/color emphasis
- No status indicators, progress bars, or completion percentages
- No navigation patterns (tabs, breadcrumbs, steppers)
- No grid systems or symmetric layouts
- No decorative elements (shadows, gradients, glow, blur)
- No information architecture (sections, categories, groupings)
- No interactive patterns (buttons, toggles, dropdowns) unless in-situ
- No dashboards, analytics, metrics, or KPIs
- No AI, predictive, or "smart" overlays
```

### Output specification module

Define what the exploration produces.

```
OUTPUT SPECIFICATION:
Produce:
1. Environmental description (how the surface feels, not how it looks)
2. Operational parameters (specific spacing, opacity, border, corner values)
3. Atmospheric quality (what the planner perceives without consciously processing)
4. Usability threshold (minimum structure required for operational tasks)
5. Translation notes (how this concept maps to Studio Matte tokens)
```

### Variable injection system

Prompts accept variables that ground exploration in specific operational contexts:

| Variable | Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `{event_type}` | string | "Wedding" |
| `{venue_name}` | string | "Rosewood Estate" |
| `{venue_count}` | number | 7 |
| `{timing}` | string | "event day, 2:15pm" |
| `{pressure_level}` | enum | calm / building / compressed / critical / decompressing / collapsed |
| `{vendor_trust}` | map | {"Elegant Catering": 22, "Sound & Light": 8, "New Videographer": 0} |
| `{active_scars}` | list | ["March gala sound delay", "cocktail bottleneck"] |
| `{planner_fatigue}` | enum | fresh / active / fatigued / exhausted / post-event |

## 4. Concept Filter System

Every concept generated through Weave passes through a 5-stage filter. Concepts must survive all 5 stages to reach Figma translation.

### Stage 1: Operational Credibility Test

**Question**: Does this concept encode real coordination pressure?

**Pass**: The concept describes something a planner actually experiences — timing compression, vendor trust, institutional memory, ceremony gravity.

**Fail**: The concept describes something intellectually interesting but operationally fictional. "Orchestration harmonics" sounds profound but doesn't map to a real coordination experience.

**Kill words**: harmonics, resonance, frequency, vibration, pulse, wave, field (unless specifically spatial), dimension, layer (unless specifically cognitive), spectrum, gradient (unless specifically opacity/spacing).

---

### Stage 2: Perceivability Test

**Question**: Would a planner actually perceive this concept in use?

**Pass**: A planner would feel the concept without being able to name it. "This venue feels familiar" (environmental memory working). "Something feels tight about this timeline" (gravity well working).

**Fail**: A planner would either not notice the concept at all (too subtle) or would notice it as a design feature (too visible). Both are failures.

**Threshold**: Subconscious perception. The concept operates below conscious processing but above imperceptible.

---

### Stage 3: Token Translatability Test

**Question**: Can this concept be expressed using existing Studio Matte tokens?

**Tokens available**: canvas (#070809), card (#121518), textPri (#e8edf2), textSec (#849eb8), teal (#4b878b), amber (#b8943f), red (#bf5050), green (#4ba073), border (#1e2530), alt (#0e1114), deep (#0a0d10), warm (#a8836a), urgCanvas (#060708), relCanvas (#0b0c0d). Plus: opacity (0-1), spacing (0-64px), corner radius (0-10px), border weight (0-2px), font weight (Regular/Medium/Semi Bold/Bold), font size (8-32px).

**Pass**: The concept maps to specific values in the token system. "Vendor trust reduces opacity from 1.0 to 0.2 over 20 events" is translatable.

**Fail**: The concept requires visual capabilities outside the token system. "Environmental texture that shifts with pressure" requires effects not available in the system.

---

### Stage 4: Usability Survival Test

**Question**: Can the planner still do their job with this concept active?

**Pass**: The planner can find information, take action, and coordinate operations. The concept reshapes HOW they experience the environment without preventing WHAT they need to do.

**Fail**: The concept is beautiful but prevents operational tasks. Object death that dissolves a vendor's phone number. Cognitive fog that hides an urgent timing conflict. Gravity wells that compress text to unreadable sizes.

**Minimum thresholds**:
- Text: minimum 9px, minimum 0.15 opacity for any operationally relevant information
- Spacing: minimum 2px between interactive elements
- Targets: minimum 32px touch target on mobile
- Readability: critical information (time, vendor name, action needed) must be findable within 5 seconds

---

### Stage 5: Anti-Drift Test

**Question**: Does this concept resist the 7 drift patterns?

| Drift Pattern | Test |
|---|---|
| Dashboard drift | Does this look like it could appear in a BI tool? Kill it. |
| SaaS drift | Does this look like it belongs in a productivity app? Kill it. |
| Sci-fi drift | Does this look like a movie UI or command center? Kill it. |
| Art-direction drift | Is this more beautiful than useful? Kill it. |
| Analytics drift | Does this display metrics, trends, or performance data? Kill it. |
| Enterprise drift | Does this look like admin tooling or CRM? Kill it. |
| Spectacle drift | Is this impressive to look at? That's suspicious. Real operational environments are not impressive. They are functional. |

## 5. Figma Translation System

### Translation workflow

```
Weave concept
    ↓
Operational parameter extraction
    ↓
Token mapping
    ↓
Surface construction (Figma Plugin API)
    ↓
Operational scenario injection
    ↓
Truth test
```

### Step 1: Operational parameter extraction

From every surviving concept, extract specific parameters:

| Parameter | Source | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Spacing values | Gravity well compression | 14px → 2px approaching ceremony |
| Opacity values | Cognitive fog zones | 1.0 active → 0.2 fog → 0.1 ghost |
| Border values | Pressure encoding | 2px red (scar) → 0.5px border (stable) → 0px (metabolized) |
| Corner radius | Temporal erosion | 2px (new/rough) → 6px (trusted/smooth) |
| Font weight | Attention density | Medium (pressure) → Regular (ambient) |
| Canvas color | Atmospheric condition | urgCanvas (compressed) → canvas (normal) → relCanvas (release) |
| Layout proportions | Operational attention | 920/520 (attention-heavy/peripheral) |

### Step 2: Token mapping

Map extracted parameters to Studio Matte tokens. If a parameter doesn't map to an existing token, the concept requires a new token — which must be justified operationally.

New token justification template:
```
Token: [name]
Value: [hex/px/number]
Operational source: [what real coordination experience produces this]
Alternative: [could an existing token handle this?]
Drift risk: [does this token enable SaaS/dashboard/spectacle patterns?]
```

### Step 3: Surface construction

Build surfaces using the established Figma Plugin API pattern (mkF/mkT helper functions). Surfaces are always:

| Viewport | Width | Height | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Desktop | 1440px | 900px | `primaryAxisSizingMode='FIXED'` |
| Tablet | 1024px | 768px | `primaryAxisSizingMode='FIXED'` |
| Mobile | 390px | 844px | `primaryAxisSizingMode='FIXED'` |

### Step 4: Operational scenario injection

Every surface must contain real operational content. No lorem ipsum. No placeholder text. Every item is a real coordination moment from a plausible event scenario.

Required content anchors:
- Real vendor names with real service types
- Real timing (specific times, not "morning/afternoon")
- Real dependencies (vendor A needs venue area before vendor B)
- Real institutional memory (specific scars from specific past events)
- Real planner actions (confirm, coordinate, call, adjust)

### Step 5: Truth test

Every completed surface undergoes the final truth test:

```
1. Cover the surface name and annotations.
2. Show the surface to someone.
3. Ask: "What is this?"

PASS answers:
- "Some kind of event coordination thing?"
- "Looks like backstage planning or something"
- "I don't know, but it feels like work — not software"

FAIL answers:
- "A nice dark-mode dashboard"
- "Some kind of app"
- "Beautiful UI design"
- "A premium SaaS product"
```

## 6. Mobile Orchestration Framework

### Core principle

Mobile is not compressed desktop. Mobile is a different cognitive instrument — a coordination awareness device carried during active orchestration.

### Mobile atmospheric modes

| Mode | When | Feel | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glance | Walking backstage, quick check | What needs me NOW? | Single gravity well at full density, everything else fog |
| Coordinate | Standing, actively managing | Active vendor/timing coordination | 2-3 items at full density, progressive fog below |
| Monitor | Seated, watching flow | Environmental awareness | Medium density throughout, weather-like atmospheric reading |
| Debrief | Post-event, reviewing | What happened, what to remember | Collapsed environment, institutional memory forming |

### Mobile-specific atmospheric patterns

**Gravity stream**: Instead of a gravity well (which implies spatial area), mobile has a gravity stream — a single-column flow where spacing compresses approaching the critical moment, then releases after. The screen is a vertical cross-section through the gravity field.

**Fog curtain**: On desktop, fog is lateral (trusted vendors in a side panel at low opacity). On mobile, fog is vertical — items below the active zone progressively dissolve downward. The planner scrolls down into fog, not across to it.

**Weather strip**: A minimal atmospheric indicator that communicates coordination state without being a status bar. Not text. Not icons. Environmental treatment of the top edge of the screen — canvas color, border weight, spacing density of the first visible element.

**Touch gravity**: Interactive elements (call vendor, confirm timing, mark complete) exist only within the gravity stream — at the point of maximum operational density. No persistent action bar. Actions appear when operational context warrants them.

### Mobile anti-patterns

- Fixed headers with navigation (this is app behavior)
- Bottom tab bars (this is dashboard behavior)
- Pull-to-refresh (this is feed behavior)
- Swipe gestures for navigation (this is card-deck behavior)
- Any pattern that says "you are using a phone app"

### Mobile truth test

Hold the phone at arm's length. Squint. What do you perceive?

**Pass**: Dense zone at top, progressively dissolving downward. Feels like a weather reading or coordination instrument.

**Fail**: Cards stacked vertically. Looks like a mobile app.

## 7. Final Truth Tests

### Test 1: The designer test

Show the surface to a UI designer. If they say "beautiful design system," the sprint failed. If they say "I'm not sure what this is, but it feels real," it is approaching the target.

### Test 2: The planner test

Show the surface to an event planner. If they start trying to use it — looking for their vendor, checking timing, scanning for problems — the environment is working. If they admire it, it is design, not environment.

### Test 3: The screenshot test

Take a screenshot. Post it on Dribbble. If it would get likes, the sprint failed. Operational environments are not photogenic. They are functional.

### Test 4: The memory test

Look at the surface for 30 seconds. Close it. What do you remember? If you remember the layout, the design is too visible. If you remember the operational situation (ceremony in 2 hours, lighting vendor is late, caterer is confirmed), the environment is communicating.

### Test 5: The substitution test

Replace the operational content with different event content (corporate conference instead of wedding). Does the environment still feel operationally real? If yes, the atmospheric system is working independent of content. If not, the realism was in the content, not the environment.

### Test 6: The mobile carry test

Imagine carrying this phone while walking backstage at a live event. Does the screen feel like it gives you operational awareness? Or does it feel like you need to stop walking and "use the app"? Operational environments support ambient cognition. Apps demand focused attention.

### Test 7: The institutional test

Compare the surface for a first-time event at a new venue versus a seventh event at a familiar venue. Do they feel different? Not through labels or badges — through environmental treatment. If the 7th event feels settled, worn-in, atmospherically dense with institutional memory — and the 1st event feels spatially fresh, uniform, without atmospheric weight — the institutional system is working.

## Exploration Sequencing

### Phase 1: Divergent generation (Lanes 1-7 in parallel)

Run all 7 exploration lanes simultaneously. Each lane produces 3-5 raw concepts. Total: 21-35 raw atmospheric concepts.

### Phase 2: Concept filtering (5-stage filter)

Pass all raw concepts through the 5-stage filter. Expected survival rate: 15-25%. Total: 4-8 surviving concepts.

### Phase 3: Cross-lane synthesis

Examine surviving concepts across lanes. Where do they overlap? Gravity wells + cognitive fog = gravity-induced dissolution (items dissolve as they enter a well's peripheral field). Environmental memory + pressure weather = institutional climate (venues carry weather patterns from accumulated experience).

### Phase 4: Parameter extraction

Extract specific visual parameters from each surviving concept. Map to Studio Matte tokens. Identify any gaps requiring new tokens.

### Phase 5: Surface construction

Build Figma surfaces using extracted parameters. Minimum 3 viewports per concept (desktop, tablet, mobile). Inject real operational scenarios.

### Phase 6: Truth testing

Apply all 7 truth tests to every surface. Discard surfaces that fail any test. Iterate surviving surfaces.

### Phase 7: Doctrine integration

Surviving, tested concepts are documented in the sprint doctrine and added to the cumulative design system.

## Anti-Drift Safeguards

### Continuous drift monitoring

Every exploration session checks for 7 drift vectors:

| Vector | Signal | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Dashboard | Surfaces start showing metrics, percentages, or trend data | Strip all quantitative display. Replace with atmospheric encoding. |
| SaaS | Surfaces start looking like productivity software | Remove all navigation, headers, action bars, and organized sections. |
| Sci-fi | Surfaces start looking futuristic or cinematic | Remove all glow, particle, blur, or environmental effects. Return to flat surfaces with spacing/opacity variation only. |
| Art-direction | Surfaces start looking beautiful or impressive | Actively make the surface less visually appealing. Operational environments are not impressive. |
| Analytics | Surfaces start analyzing or summarizing operational data | Remove all aggregation, summary, or analytical display. Show raw operational state. |
| Enterprise | Surfaces start looking like admin panels or CRM | Remove all data tables, status columns, and role-based views. |
| Spectacle | Surfaces start impressing viewers | This is the most dangerous drift. If anyone says "wow" — investigate what is producing the reaction and remove it. |

### Drift recovery protocol

If drift is detected:

1. Stop exploration immediately
2. Re-read the operational context (real planner, real event, real backstage)
3. Remove all visual elements that produce admiration
4. Rebuild from the operational question: "What does the planner need to perceive?"
5. Use only spacing, opacity, and border weight to communicate
6. Resume exploration

### The ugliness principle

An operationally correct surface may be visually unremarkable. That is not a problem. It is a feature. The planner does not admire their coordination environment. They inhabit it. Admiration indicates distance. Inhabitation indicates success.

## Operational Realism Anchors

Every Weave session begins by grounding in operational reality:

### Anchor 1: The backstage walk

Imagine walking backstage at a luxury wedding, 2 hours before ceremony. You pass the loading dock (catering delivery happening), the prep kitchen (arrangements being staged), the bridal suite (hair/makeup in progress), the sound booth (engineer running levels), and the ceremony space (chairs being placed). What do you perceive? Not a dashboard. Not a status board. An environment with varying density, pressure, and atmospheric quality. That is the target.

### Anchor 2: The stage manager's prompt book

A stage manager's prompt book is not designed. It is accumulated. Each page carries marks, notes, corrections, cross-outs from previous performances. The information density varies — sparse in stable scenes, dense in complex transitions. The book does not present information. It carries operational sediment. That is the reference for environmental memory.

### Anchor 3: The hotel concierge desk

A luxury hotel concierge desk at 6pm on a Saturday. Multiple events happening. A wedding in the ballroom. A corporate dinner in the garden room. A private party in the lounge. The concierge perceives all three simultaneously — not through a dashboard, but through environmental awareness of staff movement, timing pressure, guest flow, vendor coordination. That simultaneous ambient awareness is the target for nonlinear cognitive flow.

### Anchor 4: The air traffic coordination display

Not the interface — the pressure. Multiple approaches, variable weather, dependency chains, timing windows closing. The controller perceives urgency through spatial compression — aircraft converging on the same point at the same time. That spatial convergence (not the display) is the reference for gravity wells.

## Summary

Weave is the engine that generates concepts too atmospheric for conventional design thinking, filters them for operational credibility, and translates survivors into Figma surfaces that feel inhabited rather than designed.

The system works when:
- Explorations produce concepts no UI design process would generate
- Filters kill concepts that are intellectually interesting but operationally fictional
- Translation produces surfaces that planners want to use, not admire
- Mobile feels like carrying operational weather, not using a phone app
- The environment feels like it existed before the planner arrived

The system fails when:
- Explorations produce conventional UI under atmospheric language
- Filters pass concepts because they sound profound
- Translation produces beautiful surfaces that are operationally useless
- Mobile feels like a responsive dashboard
- A designer says "beautiful"
